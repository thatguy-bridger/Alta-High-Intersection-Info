The safe system approach

People make mistakes.
The road shouldn't punish them for it.

Traditional traffic engineering puts the burden of safety entirely on human behavior — obey the speed limit, stop completely, yield when required. The Safe System approach, now adopted by UDOT and the federal government, starts from a different premise: people will always make errors, and we should design roads that don't turn those errors fatal.

The two most powerful levers are reducing vehicle speed (less kinetic energy in a crash) and eliminating conflict points (fewer chances for crashes to happen at all). Both are achieved by a roundabout.

At 40 mph, a pedestrian struck by a vehicle has approximately a 85% chance of dying. At 20 mph, that drops to under 10%. Slowing cars before the intersection isn't a minor improvement — it's the difference between a close call and a fatality.

What "crash cost" means This is a societal metric — not just repair bills. It includes emergency response, traffic investigation time, medical care, rehabilitation, lost wages, and the statistical value of life. Federal signal warrant analysis

Zero of seven warrants met.

The Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices (MUTCD) defines seven specific conditions under which a traffic signal may be appropriate. An intersection must meet at least one. This intersection meets none. Installing a signal here would conflict with federal engineering guidance.

Proposed safety enhancements

Built for students, parents,
and everyday drivers.

A roundabout alone isn't the full plan. These school-specific safety features are built into the recommendation.

Pedestrian fencing
Guides students to marked crosswalks when school lets out, preventing free-crossing through the roundabout at peak times.
Rectangular rapid flashing beacons (RRFBs)
Driver-activated LED beacons at each crosswalk. When a pedestrian presses to cross, high-intensity lights flash to alert approaching vehicles.
Z-crossing configuration
Median islands physically turn pedestrians mid-crossing so they face oncoming traffic before completing the second half — a proven visibility improvement.
Deflection curves
Approach geometry forces drivers to curve slightly before entering the roundabout, physically reducing speed without requiring driver compliance.
Advance warning signage
Large roundabout-ahead signs on 110th South (the downhill approach) where drivers won't see the intersection until they're close.
Crossing guards retained
This intersection already qualifies for a crossing guard under state criteria. That remains unchanged — and the roundabout's lower speeds make their job safer.

Committee recommendation

Build the roundabout.

The MIST committee reached consensus after reviewing all available data. The roundabout is the only option that improves safety by every measurable standard, meets grant requirements already in place, and does so at no additional cost to the city compared to a signal.

  • 75% reduction in vehicle conflict points — from 32 to 8
  • 88–90% reduction in serious injury and fatal crashes (national data)
  • $6.7M projected savings over 20 years vs. current intersection
  • 37% reduction in emissions next to a school campus
  • Grant already secured — city match equivalent to signal cost
  • Zero federal signal warrants met — signal not indicated at this location
  • Six dedicated school-adjacent safety features included in design
